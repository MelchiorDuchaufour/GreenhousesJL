The bare envelope problem

A bare greenhouse has no heating, no crop, no ventilation. Its temperature results from three competing fluxes:

Steady-state balance

Q_solar + Q_ground = Q_LW,sky + Q_conv

In December Brussels, LW radiation to the cold sky (−30 W/m²) exceeds solar gain (+12 W/m²). The greenhouse is colder than outdoor — the glass cover subcools below T_out because it radiates to T_sky ≈ −1°C while outdoor air is at +9°C.

Bot + Balemans = de facto standard in European greenhouse climate modelling for 40+ years. All Wageningen models — the world reference in greenhouse science — use this pair. Validated across Dutch, Mediterranean, and tropical climates (Vanthoor 2011: 1-3°C RMSE vs field measurements).

Why greenhouse-specific?

Bot (1983) — exterior

Measured on inclined glass saw-tooth roofs at Wageningen. Smooth surface, open-field wind exposure. The cos(φ) tilt correction is specific to greenhouse roof geometry.

Balemans (1989) — interior

Measured inside greenhouses — large open volume, smooth glass, low ceiling, still air. Bidirectional floor model captures day/night reversal. The +1.0 minimum reflects empirical reality: h never drops below 1 W/(m²K) in a greenhouse.

TARP / ISO 15099 — building / window

TARP: furnished rooms, rough walls, higher ceilings (Walton 1983). ISO 15099: window panes in rooms, H~1-2m (ISO standard 2003). Neither was calibrated for greenhouse geometry.

No direct comparison exists in literature between Bot+Balemans and TARP/ISO 15099 for greenhouses. They represent two design philosophies: greenhouse-specific (European) vs building-generic (US/ASHRAE). Our EMS experiment (slide 10) provides this missing comparison.

Interior LW exchange: why it matters for walls

The missing heat loss path

In a greenhouse with glass walls, the floor can radiate to the wall, which then radiates to the cold sky. This indirect path is captured by EP's ScriptF but missed by Julia's simplified floor↔cover model.

Three approaches to interior LW

1. Simplified (Vanthoor 2011, Julia default)
Q = A·ε_flr·ε_cov·σ·(T⁴_flr − T⁴_cov)

Floor↔cover only, F=1 (infinite parallel plates). No wall interaction. Fast but misses wall radiative path.

2. ScriptF (EnergyPlus)
Q_i = Σ_j ScriptF_ij · σ · (T⁴_i − T⁴_j)

Hottel grey-body exchange factors. Pre-computed view factors + multi-reflection. All N surfaces coupled. Captures floor→wall→sky path.

3. Radiosity (Julia optional, GreenLight)
(I − ρ·F)·J = ε·σ·T⁴  →  q_net = A·ε/(1−ε)·(σT⁴ − J)

Mathematically equivalent to ScriptF. Solves a 6×6 linear system per timestep. Available in Julia when walls are enabled.

Adding objects to the greenhouse

How it works

# Define a heating pipe as a surface
pipe = make_surface("pipe",
  [SVector(1,1,0.5), SVector(4,1,0.5),
   SVector(4,1.1,0.5), SVector(1,1.1,0.5)];
  ε=0.9, α_solar=0.3,
  capacity=4186*0.5)  # water thermal mass

# Add to greenhouse surfaces
surfaces = savona_surfaces()
push!(surfaces, pipe)

# Rebuild enclosure → auto VF + radiosity
enc = build_enclosure(surfaces)

Adding a surface automatically recomputes all view factors (MC) and rebuilds the radiosity matrix. The new surface participates in LW exchange, receives beam solar, and has its own temperature state in the ODE.

What you can add

Heating pipes

Horizontal cylinders → rectangular approximation. High ε, thermal mass from water content. Absorbs floor LW, radiates to cover.

Growing benches / tables

Horizontal surfaces at ~0.8m height. Intercepts beam solar (shades floor). Changes the floor→cover VF.

Thermal screens

Horizontal surface between air and roof. Blocks LW from cover→floor. τ_solar = 0.3-0.7 depending on fabric.

PV panels (interior)

Tilted surfaces. Block beam solar, generate heat + electricity. Shadow pattern on floor computed from sun angle.

13

Root cause: interior solar distribution

Julia (Vanthoor model)

Transmitted solar → 100% to floor
Floor absorbs 93%, reflects 7% (lost)
Wall solar via separate wall_solar_gain()
→ Floor stores solar in thermal mass
→ Slowly releases to air over hours

EP (FullExterior)

Transmitted solar → geometric projection
Beam projected onto floor + walls by sun angle
In summer: high sun → mostly floor
At low angles: beam hits opposite wall
→ Solar on walls = rapid loss (thin glass, no mass)
→ Walls radiate to sky immediately

Why it matters for small greenhouses

For Savona (5×5m, walls=60%): EP projects a significant fraction of beam solar onto the tall glass walls. These walls have negligible thermal mass — the solar energy passes through to the exterior and is lost. Julia puts it all on the floor, where it's stored. This is the dominant source of the +1 to +7°C seasonal gap.

Fresnel: wavelength independence confirmed

Glass refractive index vs wavelength

Bandλ (nm)nτ(0°)f(75°)
PAR4001.5300.9160.643

n varies by ±1% from PAR to NIR. The angular correction f(θ) varies by <0.5% across the spectrum. A single n = 1.526 is a valid approximation.

Where the spectral difference is

Fresnel reflection (angle-dependent): nearly identical for all wavelengths. Single n is fine.
Glass absorption (wavelength-dependent): Fe₂O₃ content absorbs more NIR than PAR. This is why Julia uses different τ₀:
  • τ_PAR₀ = 0.89 (less absorption)
  • τ_NIR₀ = 0.87 (more absorption)
The spectral split is correctly handled by different τ₀ per band, not by different Fresnel n.
Conclusion: Julia's single-n Fresnel + per-band τ₀ correctly captures both the angular (Fresnel) and spectral (absorption) effects of glass. No improvement needed.

Next: geometry-based interior objects

The geometry engine

Define any object by its 3D vertices. View factors, solar projection, and radiosity follow automatically.

# Add a heating pipe
pipe = make_surface("pipe",
  [SVector(1,1,0.5), SVector(4,1,0.5),
   SVector(4,1.1,0.5), SVector(1,1.1,0.5)];
  ε=0.9, α_solar=0.3, capacity=4186*0.5)

# Add a growing bench
bench = make_surface("bench",
  [SVector(0.5,0.5,0.8), SVector(4.5,0.5,0.8),
   SVector(4.5,4.5,0.8), SVector(0.5,4.5,0.8)];
  ε=0.9, α_solar=0.6, capacity=1000*2)

surfaces = savona_surfaces()
push!(surfaces, pipe, bench)
enc = build_enclosure(surfaces)

MC view factors (N=50k) computed once at setup. Radiosity solved at each ODE step. Beam solar projected from sun angle onto each opaque surface.

Why this matters

Design scenarios

Compare insulation on north wall vs south wall. Add thermal screens at night. Evaluate PV panel positions inside the greenhouse.

Accurate thermal mass

Pipes, benches, and soil trays add thermal inertia that damps temperature swings. The geometry engine captures their radiation exchange with the envelope.

From Modelica to production

The original Vanthoor library had 3 fixed surfaces (cover, air, floor). We now support N arbitrary surfaces with exact geometry — while keeping the same greenhouse-calibrated convection physics (Bot + Balemans).

Validated against: analytical cube (0.007% error), Savona box (row sums = 1.000), EP ScriptF view factors (3% match on F(floor→cover)).
