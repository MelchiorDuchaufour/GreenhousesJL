07

Changes from Modelica

Scope

Overall modifications

  • Modelica baseline: Venlo glasshouse, roof + soil + canopy + air.
  • No walls, no PV, no FSPM, slow solver.
  • Each step zooms on one change.
  • The new term is highlighted.
Step 1 of 6 · walls

4 cardinal wall nodes (N, S, E, W)

The original library has zero wall area. The Julia rewrite adds 4 independent wall states with per-cardinal Fsky, interior LW exchange, and per-wall insulation.

Ck·dTwall,k/dt = Qcnv,air→k + Qrad,int,k (radiosity) − Qcnv,k→out − Qrad,k→sky − Qrad,k→grd

Each wall can be independently insulated: insulate(walls, :N; U=0.4, thermal_mass=50000)

Savona bare: +0.5 °C winter bias vs EP (Bot vs ISO 15099 on glass)
Step 2 of 6 · cover

Cover: Bot + Fresnel τ(θ) + radiosity

hBot = (2.8 + 1.2u) / cos(φ)   u < 4 m/s

τ(θ) = τ0 · Fresnel(n=1.526)

Bot 1983: greenhouse glass roof (Wageningen). Walls: Walton h = 5.7 + 3.8u. Interior: Balemans h = 1.7|ΔT|0.33 + 1.

τ at normal: 85 %. At θ=75° (winter): ~50 %
Step 3 of 6 · deep ground BC

Deep ground boundary (10 m)

Tdeep = mean(Tout,TMY) at ≥ 10 m depth

Old: 3 °C constant at 1.3 m. Now: 8 exponential layers to 10 m, annual mean from EPW (Kusuda 1965).

Brussels 10.7 °C, Lyon 11.9 °C, Almeria 19.0 °C
Step 4 of 6 · canopy

Stomatal closure at night

rs = 5000 s/m if PAR < 5

Night λE: 90 → 2 W/m². Stomata were left open at night (Stanghellini bug). Basil Qheat: 0 → 108 W/m².

Night transpiration: 90 → 2 W/m². Heating restored: 0 → 108 W/m²
Step 5 of 6 · PV panels

PV panels: mounted on top of glass

Ieff = [(1 − c) + c · τPV] · Iglob

Opaque (τ=0): full shade. Semi (τ=0.4): partial. Transparent (τ=0.65): max light. Panels sit on top of glass. Exterior: PV face (ε=0.90, +30% hext). Interior: glass unchanged.

Facade: roof, south_wall, north_wall. Waste heat → glass below.
Step 6 of 6 · solver

50 to 100× speedup

Tsit5 (5th-order adaptive RK) + JIT compilation + in-memory weather interpolation

Same equations, written directly in Julia and compiled once on first call. The OpenModelica path goes through Modelica → C → binary at every run.

7-day simulation: 250 ms (OMC) → 4 ms (Julia)
08

Cover energy balance

Ccov · dTcov/dt = + Qsol + Qcnv,air + Qrad,int + Qpv − Qcnv,out − Qrad,sky − Qrad,grd
Cover energy balance

PV integration and radiosity

Dominant night loss: radiation to the sky (~30 W/m²).

Unlike Modelica, Q_rad,int uses an N-surface radiosity enclosure (floor + walls + cover, view-factor coupled).

Term 1 of 6
Qsol
W · solar absorbed by glass

Qsol = αcov(θ) · Iglob · A

Glass absorbs ~3% of incoming solar. Fresnel angular correction (n=1.526) reduces τ at high incidence. PAR/NIR spectral split with multi-bounce redistribution.

Term 2 of 6
Qcnv,air
W · air → cover convection

Qcnv,air = hint · (Tair − Tcov) · A

Free convection inside the greenhouse (Balemans 1989). hint ≈ 1.7 · |ΔT|0.33 on the inclined cover.

Term 3 of 6 updated
Qrad,int
W · interior radiosity (N-surface)

(I − ρF)·J = ε·σ·T4 → qnet,cov

N-surface grey-body radiosity enclosure (floor, cover, 4 walls). Replaces the simplified floor→cover pair. Validated: cube 0.007% error, row sums = 1, energy conserved.

Term 4 of 7 updated
Qcnv,out
W · cover → outside convection

Qcnv,out = [(1−c)·hBot + c·hPV] · (Tcov − Tout) · A

Bot (1983) on bare glass, +30% roughness on PV-covered area. Tilted PV: wind-directional h (Sharples 1998) + cavity gap (Hollands 1976). See PV convection slide.

Term 5 of 7
Qrad,sky
W · cover → sky IR

Qrad,sky = εeff·Fsky·σ·A·(Tcov4 − Tsky4)

Dominant night loss (~30 W/m²). With PV: εeff = (1−c)·0.84 + c·0.90. Tilted PV changes Fsky.

Fsky = (1+cosφ)/2 (Duffie & Beckman 2013). εglass = 0.84 (Vanthoor 2011). εPV = 0.90 (Faiman 2008).

Term 7 of 7
Qrad,grd
W · cover → ground IR

Qrad,grd = εcov·(1−Fsky)·σ·A·(Tcov4 − Tout4)

Cover radiates IR downward to the ground. The original library lumped this into the sky term, overcooling tilted surfaces.

09

Air energy balance

Cair · dTair/dt = Qcnv,flr + Qsol,air + Qheat − Qcnv,cov − Qcnv,wall − Qcan − Qvent
Air energy balance

Same principle as in modelica

The indoor air as the same air node mechanism as in modelica, we just integrate the wall losses/gain.

Term 1 of 7
Qcnv,flr
W · floor → air convection

Qcnv,flr = hflr · (Tflr − Tair) · A

Bidirectional Balemans 1989: hup = 1.7·|ΔT|0.33 when the floor is hotter than the air, hdown = 1.3·|ΔT|0.25 when it is colder.

Term 2 of 7
Qsol,air
W · obstacles → air

Qsol,air = ηglob,air · Iglob · (τcov·ηPAR + (αcan+αflr)·ηNIR) · A

10 % of the incoming solar is absorbed by greenhouse construction obstacles (trusses, gutters, dust, internal pipes) and re-released as a sensible heat source to the air rather than reaching the floor.

Term 3 of 7
Qheat
W · PI controller demand

Qheat = clip0..Qmax(Kp·e + Kp/Ti·∫e·dt)

Identical PI in OMC and EP, ideal continuous mode. Realistic mode adds dead band, part-load degradation and a startup ramp.

Term 4 of 7
Qcnv,cov
W · air → cover

Qcnv,cov = hint · (Tair − Tcov) · A

Free convection on the inclined cover. hint = 1.7·|ΔT|0.33·cos(φ)−0.66.

Term 5 of 7 new
Qcnv,wall
W · air → walls

Qcnv,wall = (1.7·|ΔT|0.33 + 1) · Awalls · (Tair − Twall)

The original Modelica library has zero wall area. For a small Savona greenhouse the walls are 60 % of the envelope and dominate the loss.

Savona bare: +0.5 °C bias vs EP (winter), wall-dominated envelope
Term 6 of 7
Qcan
W · canopy sensible

Qcan = ρcp/ra · LAI · (Tair − Tcan)

At night Tcan < Tair, so the air loses heat to the canopy. With dense crops this can save 15-20 % of the heating energy by buffering day-time gains.

Term 7 of 7
Qvent
W · air → outside via vents

Qvent = ρcp · fvent(U, ΔT, u) · (Tair − Tout) · A

Boulard & Baille 1995 natural ventilation model: combined buoyancy and wind effects, gated by a T+RH controller (Körner & Challa 2003).

10

Canopy energy balance

Ccan · dTcan/dt = Qsol,can − H − λE − Qrad,can→cov
Canopy energy balance

4 fluxes through the leaves

Thin slab, low thermal mass. Absorbs PAR, transpires, radiates to cover.

Stomata gate λE: bug fix on rs changed basil heating from 0 to 108 W/m².

Term 1 of 4
Qsol,can
W · PAR + NIR absorbed

Qsol,can = PAR · 0.85 · (1 − e−K·LAI) + NIRmulti-layer

Beer-Lambert intercept on PAR plus the Modelica multi-layer NIR composition. K is the open question: in Modelica K_PAR = 0.7 and K_NIR = 0.27 are constants, but ceptometer measurements show K varies with growth stage and row geometry from ~0.45 to 0.71.

Term 2 of 4
H
W · sensible heat to air

H = ρcp/ra · LAI · (Tcan − Tair)

Negative at night when Tcan < Tair: the canopy then receives heat from the air. Drives a 3 to 5 °C night depression below the air temperature.

Term 3 of 4 fixed
λE
W · latent / Stanghellini

λE = LAI · (Δ·Rn + ρcp·VPD/ra) / (Δ + γ(1 + rs/ra))  [W/m²]

rs = 5000 s/m below 5 µmol PAR (closed stomata at night), rs = rs0(1 + 200/(PAR+50)) during the day.

Basil λE night: 90 W/m² → 2 W/m²
Term 4 of 4
Qrad,can→cov
W · IR loss to cover

Qrad,can→cov = εcan·εcov·σ·A·(Tcan4 − Tcov4) · FF

FF = 1 − e−0.94·LAI is the canopy view factor. The cold cover at night drives this loss and creates the Tcan depression observed in greenhouses.

11

Water vapor balance overview

Three coupled vapor balances drive the humidity inside the greenhouse. Each component exchanges water vapor with the air node.

Canopy transpiration
E = (Δ·Rn + ρ·cp·VPD/ra) / λ(Δ+γ(1+rs/ra))
Stanghellini formulation + night stomatal closure (rs = 5000 s/m if PAR < 5).
Cover condensation
MVAirCov = VEC · (VPair − VPsat(Tcov))
Vapor → liquid on cold glass. Releases λ·MV (10–25 W/m²) to cover energy balance.
Air vapor balance
V·dVPair/dt = MVCanAir + MVEvap − MVAirCov − MVAirOut
Transpiration + floor/substrate evap. (sources) − condensation − ventilation (sinks).
Energy coupling
VEC = 6.4×10⁻⁹ · HEC  |  λE cools canopy, λ·MV heats cover
Latent ↔ sensible heat coupling links water and energy balances.
11a

Cover condensation model

dmw/dt = MVAirCov | Qlat,cov = λvap·MVAirCov
Cover vapor side

Vapor deposits on the glass

Whenever the cover sits below the dewpoint of the indoor air, vapor diffuses to the glass and condenses. The cover is the dominant vapor sink at night.

Two effects: a vapor flux out of the air (slide 12) and a latent-heat source on the cover energy balance (slide 8).

Term 1 of 2
MVAirCov
kg s−1 · air → cover

MVAirCov = VECAirCov · max(0, Pv,air − Pv,cov)

VECAirCov = 6.4 × 10−9 · HECAirCov (Bot 1983)

Saturation-pressure-driven diffusion. Bot (1983) ties the vapor exchange coefficient to the convective heat exchange coefficient via a constant Lewis-number ratio. Forced to zero when the cover is above the dewpoint (no evaporation back).

Term 2 of 2
+ λ · MVAirCov
W · latent heat released on the cover

Qlat,cov = λvap · MVAirCov

Each kilogram of vapor that turns to liquid on the glass releases λvap ≈ 2.45 MJ of latent heat directly into the cover energy balance (slide 8). Typical magnitude: 10 to 25 W/m² on a winter night, the same order as the radiative loss to the sky.

Couples slide 8 (cover energy) and slide 12 (air vapor)
11b

Air vapor balance

Vair · dCv,air/dt = + MVCanAir − MVAirOut − MVAirTop − MVAirCov − MVAirScr
Air vapor balance

5 paths in and out of the air vapor

The indoor air is the central node of the vapor balance, just as it was for energy. One source (canopy transpiration), four sinks (ventilation, top compartment, cover and screen condensation).

Driven by the difference between the air vapor pressure and the partner surface or volume.

Term 1 of 5
MVCanAir
kg s−1 · canopy → air

MVCanAir = (λE)Stanghellini / λvap

The Stanghellini latent flux from slide 10, converted from W to kg/s by dividing by λvap ≈ 2.45 MJ/kg. Closes the loop between the canopy energy balance (latent cooling of the leaves) and the air vapor balance (vapor source).

Term 2 of 5
MVAirOut
kg s−1 · air → outside via vents

MVAirOut = fvent(U, ΔT, u) · (Cv,air − Cv,out)

Same Boulard & Baille 1995 ventilation rate as the energy balance (slide 9), now applied to the vapor concentration difference. In daytime ventilation can carry away half the canopy transpiration before it reaches the cover.

Term 3 of 5
MVAirTop
kg s−1 · air → air_top

MVAirTop = fscr(SC, ΔT) · (Cv,air · SC) · A

When the thermal screen is deployed, the air below it and the air above it become two separate vapor compartments. fscr follows the De Zwart 1996 leakage law and depends on the screen aperture SC.

Term 4 of 5
MVAirCov
kg s−1 · air → cover

MVAirCov = 6.4 × 10−9 · HECAirCov · max(0, Pv,air − Pv,cov)

Cover condensation, detailed on slide 11. The dominant vapor sink at night, around 10 to 25 W/m² of latent heat released on the cover.

Term 5 of 5
MVAirScr
kg s−1 · air → screen

MVAirScr = SC · 6.4 × 10−9 · HECAirScr · (Pv,air − Pv,scr)+

Same Bot 1983 form as the cover term, applied to the screen surface, weighted by the screen closure SC. Active when the screen sits below the air dewpoint, mostly under deployed energy screens.

11c

Transpiration: Stanghellini model

MVCanAir = LAI · (Δ·Rn + ρcp·VPD/ra) / (Δ + γ·(1 + rs/ra)) · 1/λvap
Canopy vapor side

Stanghellini, three resistances

Stanghellini 1987 is the Penman-Monteith equation rewritten for a greenhouse canopy. Three quantities decide how much vapor leaves the leaves: the available radiative energy, the aerodynamic resistance ra, and the stomatal resistance rs.

It is the same formula that drives the λE term on slide 10, divided by λvap to land in kg s−1.

Term 1 of 3 · numerator
Δ·Rn + ρcp·VPD/ra
W/m² · energy + atmospheric demand

Rn = RPAR,can + RNIR,can − Qrad,can→cov

The numerator is the sum of two driving forces: the net radiation Rn available at the leaf surface, and the air-side vapor demand ρcp·VPD/ra driven by the vapor pressure deficit. Δ is the slope of the saturation curve at Tcan.

Term 2 of 3 · resistances
Δ + γ·(1 + rs/ra)
· aerodynamic and stomatal control

ra from canopy aerodynamics, rs from Vialet-Chabrand / Medlyn

ra is the boundary-layer resistance around the leaf (small in well-ventilated greenhouses, ~100 s/m). rs is the stomatal resistance, dynamic in PlantBiophysics, fixed in the Modelica baseline. γ is the psychrometric constant.

Term 3 of 3 · vapor flux night fix
MVCanAir
kg s−1 m−2 · vapor leaving the canopy

MVCanAir = λE / λvap

Night-time rs fix: when PAR < 5 µmol m−2 s−1, rs is forced to 5000 s/m, which drastically increases the stomatal resistance and brings λE from 90 W/m² down to 2 W/m² on the LAI = 4 canopy.

Closes the loop with slide 12, term 1

PV panel convection model

Panel on top of glass: two-sided convection

Top Exposed to wind + sky. htop from Sharples (1998): wind direction dependent.
Gap Cavity between PV and glass. Hollands (1976): Nu–Ra for inclined sealed gaps.
Glass Interior face keeps its usual Balemans (1989) h and εglass: the PV sits on the outside, so the indoor LW exchange with floor/walls/canopy is the same as without PV.

Equations

Top face: Sharples & Charlesworth (1998)

htop = 1.3 · √(hforced² + hnat²)
hforced = hBot(u) · |cos(θwind)|0.6 / cos(φpanel)

Windward: full forced. Leeward: natural dominates. 1.3 = PV roughness factor.

Gap cavity: Hollands et al. (1976)

Nu = 1 + 1.44·max(0, Ra·cosφ − 1708) + (Ra·sin 1.8φ)1/3 − 1
hcavity = Nu · kair / Hgap

Ra = g·β·ΔT·H³/(ν²)·Pr. Typical gap: 5–12 cm. At ΔT=5°C, gap=8cm: h ≈ 20 W/(m²K).

Effective cover properties with PV

εext = (1−c)·0.84 + c·0.90
hext = (1−c)·hBot + c·htop

PV coverage c = 0–1. Exterior ε blended. Interior ε = 0.84 (glass underneath, unchanged). Tilt extra ≥ 0 (panels cannot tilt below roof slope).

Cell temperature: Faiman (2008)

Tcell = Tout + Iglob / (U0 + U1·u)
η(T) = ηSTC·[1 + β·(Tcell − 25)]

U0=25, U1=6.84. Waste heat Qpv = c·α·I − Pelec → conducted to glass below.

Two driving forces

Stack effect (buoyancy)

Warm air rises out through roof vents. Driven by ΔT = Tin − Tout. Dominates at V < 1.5 m/s.

Wind effect (turbulent)

Pressure fluctuations create two-way exchange through each vent. Cw covers both steady and turbulent wind pressure. Dominates at V > 2 m/s.

Vent opening control

Temperature proportional

U = Kp · max(Tair − Ton, 0). Linear ramp from closed to fully open.

+ Humidity override (Körner & Challa 2003)

U = max(UT, URH). Opens vents when RH > 85% even if T is below setpoint. Drying the air avoids fungal disease (Botrytis) and cover dripping. Costs +5-15% heating.

Heat loss: Qvent = ρcp · f · A · ΔT  [W] |  Vapor: MVvent = A·MH2O·f/R · (VPin/Tin − VPout/Tout)  [kg/s]

Phase-change materials

PCMStorage

Paraffin or salt-hydrate layer with melting hysteresis. Stores latent heat during the day, releases it at night. Plugged into the wall or floor thermal mass node.

More common materials (XPS, rock wool, polyurethane, double/triple glazing) are already in the library, try them in the geometry webapp.

Convection coefficients

Interior convection: Balemans (1989) free convection for greenhouse enclosures. Exterior: Bot (1983) forced convection on glass roofs. Both calibrated on greenhouses only.

No specific hconv for insulation panels (rock wool, XPS). Current model uses the same greenhouse glass correlations. Extending to insulated envelopes requires dedicated correlations (ISO 6946 or measured data).

Heating and cooling equipment

Heating

CHP
Qth=ηth(PLR)·Pfuel · Pel=ηel(PLR)·Pfuel

Polynomial η(PLR), first-order start-up τ=180 s. Electricity sold or self-consumed.

Qth=η(PLR)·Pfuel

Gas / biomass. Standing + cycling losses cost 5–15% seasonal η (ASHRAE 2020).

COP=ηII·Tcond/(Tcond−Teva)

Second-law ηII≈0.4–0.5. Captures lift degradation with 2 parameters.

HeatPump_ConsoClim
Qcap=Qnom·CAPFT(Teva,Tcond) · Pel=Pnom·EIRFT·EIRFPLR(PLR)

Bi-quadratic maps (IEA Annex 42). Variable-speed part-load behaviour.

Dynamic day/night setpoints

Screen deployment

Thermal screen retracted when Iglob > threshold, deployed at night. Reduces radiative losses by 40-60%.

Temperature integration

24h average matters more than instantaneous T for crop growth. Allow T to drift within a band and compensate later → exploit free solar gains.

CO2 enrichment

CO2 mass balance

dCO2/dt = (MCinj − MCvent − MCcan) / hair
MCvent = fvent × (CO2,air − CO2,out)
MCcan = An × 44.01×10−3  (Farquhar photosynthesis)
CO2,out = 420 ppm  |  Γ* = 50 ppm (compensation point)

Three injection strategies

On/off (setpoint)

CO2 < target AND vents <30% → inject at max rate. Simple, cheap, wastes CO2 when PAR is low.

Proportional

Rate ∝ deficit from target. Smoother, less overshoot. Still a fixed target regardless of light or crop state.

Economic optimal (Pontryagin)

Inject when marginal photosynthetic return > marginal cost. Couples PAR, CO2 response curve, and ventilation losses via instantaneous Hamiltonian maximization.

Implementation
On/off CO2Enrichment(target, rate)
Proport. CO2Config(:proportional)
Economic EconomicCO2(w_crop, w_co2, w_vent)

Hamiltonian condition

∂H/∂Uc = ηc − cco2 = 0
ηc ≈ wcrop × PAR × dAn/dCO2
dAn/dCO2 = (Km + Γ*) / (CO2 + Km)²
ηc = approximate costate (marginal value of CO2)
Inject when ηc > cco2 + cvent × fvent

Weight parameters wcrop, wco2, wvent are placeholders. Calibrate against crop data or economic inputs (crop price, CO2 cost/kg).

Agrivoltaic case: motivation

Vanthoor's source-sink treats the canopy as one well-mixed LAI compartment. Fine for a flat homogeneous layer, breaks under PV rows.

  • Dense PV layouts can drop direct PAR by 40 %.
  • Heterogeneous shading changes which leaves dominate transpiration.
  • Penman-Monteith (open-field) over-estimates ET in a covered greenhouse.

Stomatal lag under flicker

  • GreenhousesJL uses steady-state Stanghellini, rs reacts instantly.
  • Real stomata have 5–30 min time constants (Vialet-Chabrand 2013).
  • Under PV shadow flicker, opening lags the light → gas exchange desynchronised, ET mis-estimated.

→ A spatially-explicit FSPM is needed to quantify the bias, then bake the result into the source-sink for production runs.

Two-level architecture: calibration and implementation

L1

Reference: VPL + PlantBiophysics.jl

  • 3D FSPM with phytomers, leaflets, fruits
  • Monte Carlo ray-tracing on the actual scene geometry
  • FvCB + Medlyn + Stanghellini per leaf
  • Heavy: minutes per simulated season
  • Role: produce reference data for any new agrivoltaic configuration, then calibrate L2.
L2

Production: GreenhousesJL crop sub-model

  • Source-sink Vanthoor / Marcelis form
  • Beer–Lambert canopy with calibrated K(LAI, age)
  • Coupled to envelope, HVAC, ventilation, CO₂
  • Fast: seconds per simulated season
  • Role: seasonal optimisation of heating setpoints and dispatch under agrivoltaic constraints.

Coupling between the two levels

Level 1 simulates a few seasons under representative weather and PV configurations, producing reference (LAI, An, Tr, biomass) trajectories. Level 2 inherits four interface variables , q̇PAR,Can, TCan, ṁv,CanAir, ṁc,Can , whose response curves are fitted to L1. Once fitted, L2 runs at the speed required for control optimisation.

Inspired by Rémi Vezy's PlantBiophysics.jl architecture and the discussions with the CIRAD/CEA team (PhD Corentin Coutellier).

Penman-Monteith vs Stanghellini

PlantBiophysics.jl uses Monteith (2013). Open-field energy balance. In a greenhouse it over-estimates Tr.

  • Ignores cover/screen/PV reduction of Rn.
  • Ignores indoor near-saturation that damps VPD.
  • Ignores canopy↔structure LW exchange.

Stanghellini (1987): built for greenhouses.

Tr = ((Rn − G)·Δ + ρa·cp·δe / ra) / (λ·(Δ + γ·(1 + rs/ra)))

  • rs: Medlyn (2011), reused from PlantBiophysics.jl.
  • Rn: includes cover/screen LW.
  • ra: still-air aerodynamics.

→ Drop-in: swap the energy balance, keep Medlyn rs.

Why it matters for K

A constant leaf angle (CONST) under-estimates light interception in the middle canopy by 16 % under diffuse light and 7 % under direct light compared to the explicit-angle model (EXPL) of Sarlikioti.

Validation: ceptometers above and through the canopy

Validation protocol (Sarlikioti 2011)

  • Multiple cultivars grown side-by-side at the same density
  • 3 horizontal positions: row centre, between rows, path centre
  • 3 vertical heights: 0.50 m (low), 1.00 m (mid), 1.75 m (above the canopy)
  • → 9 ceptometer points per scene, repeated over 6 weeks of growth
  • LAI measured destructively or by a SunScan probe to anchor the comparison

What we look for

  • Vertical decay: top → bottom drop should match measurement at every position
  • Horizontal decay: path → row drop should match (Sarlikioti measured ~50 % attenuation)
  • Cultivar discrimination: planophile (Momotaro) and erectophile (Merlice) should produce different vertical profiles, both reproduced by the FSPM
  • Slope of measured-vs-simulated close to 1 : 1 across all scenes (Sarlikioti Fig 5)

If the FSPM passes this test for several cultivars, the per-leaflet PAR distribution it produces is trustworthy enough to drive the calibration of the simplified Vanthoor crop model.

Per-leaf PAR: ray-tracing vs Beer-Lambert

  • The PAR field at floor level is strongly heterogeneous: high under the path, dark under the rows.
  • Vanthoor's K = 0.7 is a uniform attenuation that overestimates interception by ≈ 14 pp on this geometry.
  • Replacing Vanthoor's constant by the FSPM-averaged K̄ = 0.47 in Beer-Lambert collapses to the right mean (50 W/m²) but the spatial profile is still flat.
  • The averaged K̄ is what gets shipped to GreenhousesJL; the per-leaflet PAR field is what is shipped as the calibration target for a future per-leaflet FvCB coupling.

Supplementary: Farquhar photosynthesis model (FvCB)

The Farquhar, von Caemmerer & Berry (1980) model computes net CO&sub2; assimilation An as the minimum of two limiting rates.

Rubisco-limited (low light or high CO&sub2;)

Ac = Vcmax · (Ci − Γ*) / (Ci + Kc(1 + O/Ko))

RuBP-regeneration-limited (high light)

Aj = J · (Ci − Γ*) / (4Ci + 8Γ*)

Net assimilation

An = min(Ac, Aj) − Rd

  • Vcmax: maximum carboxylation rate, temperature-dependent (Arrhenius)
  • J: electron transport rate, depends on absorbed PAR via a hyperbolic response
  • Ci: intercellular CO&sub2;, linked to stomatal conductance gs (Medlyn 2011)
  • Rd: dark respiration (~1–2 µmol/m²/s at 25 °C)

Coupling to the greenhouse model

VariableFromTo
PARSolar model (Beer-Lambert)J (electron transport)
TcanCanopy energy balanceVcmax, Jmax, Γ*
CO&sub2;Air CO&sub2; balanceCi via gs
AnFvCB outputCO&sub2; sink, dry matter growth
gsMedlyn (2011) / Vialet-ChabrandλE (Stanghellini)

In GreenhousesJL, the Vanthoor crop model uses a simplified photosynthesis function, not the full FvCB. The Level 1 pipeline (VPL + PlantBiophysics.jl) runs the full FvCB per leaf, producing the reference data that calibrates Level 2.

→ FvCB is non-linear in PAR: the mean PAR gives a different An than the mean of An(PAR per leaf). This is why per-leaflet PAR from the FSPM matters for accurate canopy photosynthesis.
